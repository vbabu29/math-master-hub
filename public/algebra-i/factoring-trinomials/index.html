    Factoring Trinomials

    5 Common Mistakes When Factoring Trinomials

    Factoring trinomials is a core algebra skill, but it is also where many students get stuck. Most errors aren’t caused by “not knowing the math,” but by missing small steps that make the problem much harder than it needs to be.

    Here is the first and most frequent mistake students make.

    1. Skipping the Greatest Common Factor (GCF)

    Students often try to factor the trinomial immediately without first checking for a common factor.

    This makes the problem harder than it needs to be.

    Example

    Factor:

    3x2+15x+18 3x^2 + 15x + 18 3x2+15x+18

    ❌ Common Wrong Attempt

    A student tries to factor the trinomial directly:

    (3x+?)(x+?) (3x + ?)(x + ?) (3x+?)(x+?)

    Because the numbers are larger than necessary, it becomes harder to find the correct factors.

    ✅ Correct Solution

    First check for a Greatest Common Factor (GCF).

    Each term contains a factor of 3, so factor it out:

    3x2+15x+18=3(x2+5x+6) 3x^2+15x+18 = 3(x^2 + 5x + 6) 3x2+15x+18=3(x2+5x+6)

    Now factor the trinomial inside the parentheses:

    x2+5x+6=(x+2)(x+3) x^2+5x+6=(x+2)(x+3) x2+5x+6=(x+2)(x+3)

    Final Answer:

    3(x+2)(x+3) 3(x+2)(x+3) 3(x+2)(x+3)

    💡 Tip

    Always check for a Greatest Common Factor (GCF) before factoring a trinomial.

    Factoring out the GCF simplifies the expression and makes the remaining trinomial easier to factor.

    2. Mixing Up Positive and Negative Signs

    This mistake happens when a student finds the right numbers but assigns the plus or minus signs to the wrong binomial.

    Example

    Factor:

    x2+x−6 x^2 + x - 6 x2+x−6

    ❌ Common Wrong Attempt

    A student finds the factors 333 and 222 but assigns the negative sign to the larger number:

    (x−3)(x+2) (x - 3)(x + 2) (x−3)(x+2)

    Check: −3+2=−1-3 + 2 = -1−3+2=−1. The middle term should be +1x+1x+1x.

    ✅ Correct Solution

    The factors of −6-6−6 must add to +1+1+1. Since the sum is positive, the larger factor (333) must be positive:

    (x+3)(x−2) (x + 3)(x - 2) (x+3)(x−2)

    💡 Tip

    The “Add-Back” Test. Before moving on, mentally add your two numbers. If they don’t exactly match the middle coefficient (including the sign), your answer is wrong.

    3. Matching Multiplication but Failing Addition

    Many students find a pair of factors that multiply to the constant ccc and assume they are finished without checking the sum.

    Example

    Factor:

    x2+10x+24 x^2 + 10x + 24 x2+10x+24

    ❌ Common Wrong Attempt

    A student sees 242424 and immediately thinks of 12×212 \times 212×2:

    (x+12)(x+2) (x + 12)(x + 2) (x+12)(x+2)

    Check: While 12×2=2412 \times 2 = 2412×2=24, the sum 12+2=1412 + 2 = 1412+2=14. We need a sum of 101010.

    ✅ Correct Solution

    List all factor pairs of 242424 until the sum matches 101010:

    • 3×8=243 \times 8 = 243×8=24 (Sum: 111111)

    • 4×6=244 \times 6 = 244×6=24 (Sum: 101010)

      Final Answer: (x+4)(x+6)(x + 4)(x + 6)(x+4)(x+6)

    💡 Tip

    The Sum-Product Table. If you are stuck, draw a small table. Label one side “Product” and the other “Sum.” Don’t stop until both columns match your trinomial.

    4. Ignoring the Leading Coefficient (a≠1a \neq 1a=1)

    Students often try to use the “simple” shortcut even when the x2x^2x2 term has a coefficient other than 111.

    Example

    Factor:

    2x2+7x+3 2x^2 + 7x + 3 2x2+7x+3

    ❌ Common Wrong Attempt

    A student ignores the 222 and factors it like a simple trinomial:

    (x+1)(x+3) (x + 1)(x + 3) (x+1)(x+3)

    Check: (x+1)(x+3)=x2+4x+3(x+1)(x+3) = x^2 + 4x + 3(x+1)(x+3)=x2+4x+3. This does not match the original 2x2+7x+32x^2 + 7x + 32x2+7x+3.

    ✅ Correct Solution

    Use the AC Method. Multiply a×ca \times ca×c (2×3=62 \times 3 = 62×3=6). Find factors of 666 that add to 777 (666 and 111):

    1. Split the middle: 2x2+6x+1x+32x^2 + 6x + 1x + 32x2+6x+1x+3

    2. Factor by grouping: 2x(x+3)+1(x+3)2x(x + 3) + 1(x + 3)2x(x+3)+1(x+3)

      Final Answer: (2x+1)(x+3)(2x + 1)(x + 3)(2x+1)(x+3)

    💡 Tip

    Look at the Front. Always look at the x2x^2x2 term first. If there is a coefficient, you cannot use the simple shortcut. You must use the AC method or trial and error.

    5. Neglecting to Verify via Redistribution (FOIL)

    The final mistake is psychological: students finish the work but don’t verify if the answer actually works.

    Example

    A student factors x2+3x−10x^2 + 3x - 10x2+3x−10 and gets (x+5)(x−2)(x + 5)(x - 2)(x+5)(x−2).

    ❌ Common Mistake

    Turning in the paper without a quick 5-second check. Even a small sign error can ruin the entire problem.

    ✅ Correct Solution

    Perform a quick FOIL check (First, Outer, Inner, Last):

    • F: x⋅x=x2x \cdot x = x^2x⋅x=x2

    • O/I: −2x+5x=3x-2x + 5x = 3x−2x+5x=3x

    • L: 5⋅−2=−105 \cdot -2 = -105⋅−2=−10

      The result x2+3x−10x^2 + 3x - 10x2+3x−10 matches the original problem.

    💡 Tip

    The 5-Second FOIL. You don’t always have to write it down. At the very least, mentally multiply the “Last” terms to check ccc and the “Outer/Inner” terms to check bbb. It takes 5 seconds and saves your grade.

    Conclusion: From Mistakes to Mastery

    Factoring is more about discipline than raw talent. By checking for the GCF first, being careful with your signs, and always verifying with FOIL, you eliminate 90% of the reasons students lose points.
